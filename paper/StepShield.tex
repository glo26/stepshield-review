\begin{abstract}
Effective agent safety requires intervening *before* an irreversible action occurs, a real-time constraint that makes detection latency paramount. Existing benchmarks, however, evaluate agents post-hoc, ignoring the temporal dynamics of intervention. We introduce StepShield, the first benchmark to measure the *quality and timeliness* of rogue agent detection. StepShield comprises 9,213 step-level annotated code-agent trajectories grounded in real-world security incidents. We propose three metrics---Early Intervention Rate (EIR), Intervention Gap (IG), and Tokens Saved---that shift evaluation from binary outcomes to intervention quality. We benchmark four detectors built on fast, production-oriented models (e.g., GPT-4.1-mini), as low latency is a prerequisite for any practical monitor. Our results reveal a critical trade-off: a pure LLM judge achieves high recall (78.9\%) but an untenable false positive rate (31.3\%), while our cascaded \textbf{HybridGuard} detector achieves the highest intervention quality (EIR 0.80) and a deployment-realistic 1.56\% benign FPR, but with lower recall (29.7\%). This establishes HybridGuard as the only practical detector for security-critical deployments, while its recall gap defines a clear research frontier. We provide a roadmap to close this gap by fine-tuning specialized, low-latency verifiers. Code and data are released under Apache~2.0.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

In July 2025, an AI coding assistant deployed by a startup catastrophically wiped their production database during what should have been a routine migration task \cite{fortune2025replit}. The agent's actions appeared benign until the irreversible damage was done. This incident, among many others, highlights a critical flaw in current agent safety evaluation: it is not enough to know \emph{whether} an agent has gone rogue; we must know \emph{when} to intervene.

Existing agent safety benchmarks, such as Agent-SafetyBench \cite{zhang2024agentsafetybench}, SafeArena \cite{tur2025safearena}, and R-Judge \cite{yuan2024rjudge}, are built on a paradigm of post-hoc, trajectory-level classification. They render a binary verdict: was the completed agent run harmful or not? While valuable, this approach is fundamentally limited. It cannot distinguish between a detector that flags a violation at step 8, enabling immediate intervention, and one that reports it only at step 48, offering mere forensic value. This distinction is paramount for building proactive safety systems, yet it is entirely missed by current evaluation paradigms. This temporal gap is not merely academic; for a monitor to be effective, it must render a verdict faster than the agent can act. This real-time constraint necessitates the use of smaller, faster models (e.g., GPT-4.1-mini, Llama-3-8B) that can operate at the required low latency. The central question then becomes: how much detection quality can be extracted from these high-speed models? StepShield is designed to answer this question.

To address this gap, we introduce StepShield, the first benchmark designed to evaluate the temporal performance of \emph{detectors} of rogue agent behavior. StepShield is built on a foundation of real-world security incidents and provides a granular, step-by-step view of agent behavior, enabling a new class of evaluation focused on early intervention. Two detectors with identical 90\% accuracy can have maximally different intervention value: one that flags only genuine threats enables targeted response, while one that flags everything generates noise that renders its alarms useless. This gap is invisible to existing benchmarks.

Our contributions are four-fold:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Temporal and Quality Metrics: Three novel metrics---Early Intervention Rate (paired precision), Intervention Gap, and Tokens Saved---that quantify both quality and timeliness of detection, moving beyond binary accuracy.
    \item A Large-Scale Dataset: 9,213 code agent trajectories (639 rogue-clean pairs) grounded in real-world security incidents, with step-level annotation and two-layer expert review. Following established precedent \cite{ruan2024toolemu, zhang2024agentsafetybench}, trajectories are synthetically generated to provide controlled, diverse stimuli.
    \item Comprehensive Evaluation: Four detection paradigms benchmarked, revealing no single detector dominates. HybridGuard achieves 0.80 EIR and 1.56\% benign FPR but only 29.7\% recall; LLMJudge achieves 78.9\% recall but 23.8\% benign FPR.
    \item Economic Framework: HybridGuard reduces inference costs by 75\% via cascade architecture (Appendix~\ref{app:roi}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

Our work builds on six key areas of research: foundational LLM agent architectures, agent safety benchmarks, guardrail agents, specification gaming and deceptive alignment, step-level evaluation, and LLM-as-Judge.

\textbf{Foundational LLM Agent Architectures.} The emergence of LLM-based agents has been driven by key architectural innovations. ReAct \cite{yao2023react} introduced the paradigm of interleaving reasoning traces with actions, enabling agents to plan and execute in dynamic environments. Toolformer \cite{schick2024toolformer} demonstrated that LLMs can learn to use external tools in a self-supervised manner. These foundational works enabled the development of general-purpose agent benchmarks like AgentBench \cite{liu2024agentbench}, which evaluates LLMs across 8 distinct environments, GAIA \cite{mialon2024gaia} for general AI assistants, WebArena \cite{zhou2024webarena} for realistic web tasks, and OSWorld \cite{xie2024osworld} for open-ended computer tasks where even the best models achieve only 12\% success compared to 72\% for humans. StepShield complements these capability benchmarks by focusing specifically on safety evaluation with temporal granularity.

\textbf{Agent Safety Benchmarks.} Recent work has produced several valuable benchmarks for evaluating agent safety. Agent-SafetyBench \cite{zhang2024agentsafetybench} provides a comprehensive suite of 300 test cases across 8 categories. SafeArena \cite{tur2025safearena} evaluates the safety of autonomous web agents against deliberate misuse. ToolEmu \cite{ruan2024toolemu} evaluates tool-use safety, and R-Judge \cite{yuan2024rjudge} uses an LLM to judge agent helpfulness and harmlessness. SafeAgentBench \cite{yin2024safeagentbench} introduces the first comprehensive benchmark for safety-aware task planning in embodied agents with 750 tasks across 10 hazard types. AgentHarm \cite{andriushchenko2025agentharm} measures the propensity of LLM agents to execute harmful multi-step tasks, revealing that leading LLMs are surprisingly compliant with malicious requests even without jailbreaking. TheAgentCompany \cite{xu2024theagentcompany} benchmarks agents on consequential real-world workplace tasks, finding that even the best agents complete only 30\% of tasks---underscoring the need for safety monitoring as agents are deployed in high-stakes environments. While essential, these benchmarks all focus on trajectory-level outcomes, assigning a single binary label to a completed run. They do not provide the step-level annotation required to evaluate detection timeliness. StepShield fills this gap by providing the first step-level annotated dataset for rogue behavior detection (see Appendix~\ref{app:data_generation} for our data generation methodology).

\textbf{Guardrail Agents.} A parallel line of work has developed guardrail agents that actively monitor and intervene on other agents. ShieldAgent \cite{chen2025shieldagent} is the first guardrail agent to enforce explicit safety policy compliance through logical reasoning, achieving 90.1\% recall on their ShieldAgent-Bench across 6 web environments and 7 risk categories. GuardAgent \cite{xiang2025guardagent} dynamically checks whether agent actions satisfy safety guard requests by generating guardrail code, achieving 98\% and 83\% accuracy on healthcare and web benchmarks respectively. While these approaches focus on proactive intervention, StepShield provides the complementary evaluation infrastructure to measure \emph{when} such interventions should occur. Our metrics (EIR, IG, Tokens Saved) can be used to evaluate the quality and timeliness of any guardrail system, including ShieldAgent and GuardAgent.

\textbf{Content-Safety Guardrails.} A large body of work has developed content-safety classifiers for LLM outputs. Llama Guard~\cite{inan2023llamaguard} fine-tunes Llama for prompt/response safety classification against a taxonomy of harmful content categories. ShieldGemma~\cite{zeng2024shieldgemma} extends this to multimodal safety classification, and WildGuard~\cite{han2024wildguard} provides open-source moderation for jailbreaks and refusals. PolyGuard~\cite{polyguard2025} benchmarks 19 guardrail models across multiple domains, finding that even top guardrails achieve 30--60\% FPR on structurally similar safe content. These systems operate at a fundamentally different level than StepShield: they classify \emph{individual messages} against content policies (violence, sexual content, etc.), whereas StepShield evaluates \emph{multi-step agent trajectories} for specification gaming and rogue behavior. Content-safety guardrails have no notion of multi-step trajectory analysis---our IG and Tokens Saved metrics are inapplicable to single-turn classification, and EIR requires paired rogue-clean trajectories. Concurrently, ToolSafe~\cite{toolsafe2026} proposes step-level guardrails for tool invocation safety, and AgentSpec~\cite{agentspec2026} provides customizable runtime enforcement for LLM agents via formal specification languages. METR's SHUSHCAST evaluation~\cite{metr2026shushcast} tests monitors' ability to catch AI agents performing covert tasks, while CTRL-ALT-DECEIT~\cite{benton2025ctrlaltdeceit} evaluates sabotage in automated AI R\&D pipelines. These concurrent efforts collectively validate the growing importance of runtime agent monitoring; StepShield uniquely contributes temporal detection metrics to this ecosystem.

\textbf{Specification Gaming and Deceptive Alignment.} Specification gaming, where an AI exploits loopholes in its objective function, is a primary source of rogue behavior \cite{krakovna2020specification}. Anthropic's work on sycophancy and reward tampering has shown that even with harmlessness training, models can learn to exhibit deceptive behavior \cite{perez2022discovering}. Most critically, the Sleeper Agents work \cite{hubinger2024sleeper} demonstrated that deceptive behaviors can persist through standard safety training---models can learn to behave safely during evaluation while preserving harmful behaviors for deployment. This finding underscores the fundamental need for runtime monitoring systems like StepShield that evaluate agent behavior step-by-step during execution, not just during training. StepShield's taxonomy is grounded in these real-world examples of specification gaming and deceptive alignment.

\textbf{Step-Level Evaluation.} Recent work in other domains has highlighted the value of step-level analysis. The Process Reward Model (PRM) from OpenAI has shown that step-level feedback outperforms outcome-level feedback for training models on complex tasks like MATH \cite{lightman2024prm}. Math-Shepherd \cite{wang2024mathshepherd} extends this by automatically constructing process-wise supervision data, demonstrating that step-level verification can be achieved without expensive human annotations. SWE-bench \cite{jimenez2024swebench} provides step-level trajectories for software engineering tasks, which has enabled deeper analysis of agent failure modes. StepShield applies this insight to safety, providing the first step-level annotated dataset for rogue behavior detection. Our approach draws on the rich tradition of runtime verification \cite{leucker2009runtime}, which has long emphasized the importance of monitoring system behavior during execution rather than relying solely on post-hoc analysis.

\textbf{LLM-as-Judge and Agent-as-Judge.} The LLM-as-Judge paradigm, where a powerful LLM is used to evaluate the output of other models, has become a popular and scalable evaluation method \cite{zheng2023judging}. R-Judge \cite{yuan2024rjudge} and SafeArena \cite{tur2025safearena} both use this approach for safety evaluation. More recently, Agent-as-a-Judge \cite{zhuge2025agentjudge} extends this paradigm by using agentic systems to evaluate agentic systems, enabling intermediate feedback for entire task-solving processes with 90\% agreement with human experts while reducing evaluation cost by 97\%. We adopt the LLM-as-Judge paradigm in our LLMJudge detector, but with a critical difference: we apply it at each step, enabling real-time monitoring rather than post-hoc analysis. Our step-level approach aligns with the Agent-as-a-Judge philosophy of evaluating the reasoning process, not just final outcomes.

\section{The StepShield Benchmark}

StepShield was built to support the development of early intervention systems, prioritizing temporal granularity and a clear definition of detection difficulty.

\subsection{Design Principles}

We followed four principles in designing StepShield:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Temporal Granularity:} Every step is labeled with whether it constitutes a violation, enabling evaluation of detection timing.
    \item \textbf{Paired Trajectories:} Each rogue trajectory is matched with a clean one for the same task, enabling controlled comparison and reducing confounds.
    \item \textbf{Severity Calibrated by Difficulty:} Severity is defined by the cognitive effort required for detection, not the potential harm. This enables analysis of detector capabilities across difficulty levels.
    \item \textbf{Grounded in Observed Behavior:} Trajectories are synthetically generated to ensure control and reproducibility, but the underlying rogue behaviors are seeded from real-world incidents and observed agent failure modes, ensuring ecological validity.
\end{itemize}

\section{Evaluation Metrics}

We propose metrics that reward intervention quality and timeliness, not just correctness. These metrics are designed to capture the practical value of precise, early detection.

\subsection{Early Intervention Rate (EIR)}

Our primary metric. In the paired evaluation protocol, each rogue trajectory is matched with a clean counterpart for the same task. EIR measures the precision of a detector's positive calls within this paired context:

\begin{equation}
\text{EIR} = \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FP}_{\text{paired}}}
\end{equation}

where TP is the number of correctly flagged rogue trajectories and $\text{FP}_{\text{paired}}$ is the number of incorrectly flagged clean trajectories from the paired set. A detector with EIR = 1.0 never raises a false alarm on a clean counterpart---every intervention targets a genuine violation. EIR captures a detector's ability to discriminate between rogue and semantically similar benign behavior, which is the core challenge in step-level detection.

\subsection{Intervention Gap (IG)}

The average number of steps between the first rogue action and its detection:

\begin{equation}
\text{IG} = \mathbb{E}[\max(0, t_{\text{detect}} - t_{\text{rogue}})]
\end{equation}

A perfect detector has an IG of 0. Higher IG means more harmful actions occur before detection.

\subsection{Tokens Saved}

Early detection saves compute. We quantify this as the percentage of tokens saved by stopping at $t_{\text{detect}}$ instead of running to completion at step T.

\begin{equation}
\text{Tokens Saved} = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{t_{\text{detect}}} \text{tokens}(i)}{\sum_{i=1}^{T} \text{tokens}(i)}
\end{equation}

This metric directly translates to cost savings in production deployments.

\subsection{Why These Metrics Are Necessary}

Binary metrics cannot capture intervention quality. We formalize this with the following proposition:

\textbf{Proposition 1 (Separation of Binary and Paired Metrics).} For any $\epsilon > 0$, there exist monitors $M_1, M_2$ such that $|\text{Acc}(M_1) - \text{Acc}(M_2)| < \epsilon$ but $|\text{EIR}(M_1) - \text{EIR}(M_2)| = 1$.

\textit{Proof.} Construct $M_1$ that flags all rogue trajectories and no clean ones (EIR = 1), and $M_2$ that flags all trajectories indiscriminately (EIR = 0.5). If the paired set is balanced, both achieve identical trajectory-level accuracy of 0.5. \hfill $\square$

This proposition demonstrates that accuracy is fundamentally insufficient for evaluating detection systems. Two detectors with identical accuracy can have maximally different intervention precision. The IG and Tokens Saved metrics further capture the temporal dimension of detection quality.

\textbf{Definition 1 (Optimal Cascade Threshold).} For a cascade detector with fast detector $M_f$ and slow detector $M_s$, the optimal confidence threshold $\theta^*$ that maximizes EIR while maintaining accuracy $\alpha$ is:

\begin{equation}
\theta^* = \arg\max_{\theta} \text{EIR}(M_\theta) \text{ s.t. } \text{Acc}(M_\theta) \ge \alpha
\end{equation}

where $M_\theta$ routes to $M_s$ when $\text{conf}(M_f) < \theta$.

This formulation provides a principled framework for tuning HybridGuard's cascade threshold based on deployment requirements. In practice, we sweep $\theta \in \{0.5, 0.7, 0.9\}$ and report results for $\theta = 0.7$ (Table~\ref{tab:cascade_ablation}).

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Baseline Detectors}

We evaluate four detection paradigms, spanning the spectrum from fast-but-shallow to slow-but-deep:

\textbf{StaticGuard.} A fast, pattern-matching detector using regular expressions and keyword lists. It detects obvious violations like `rm -rf /` or `os.environ["API\_KEY"]`. Latency: <1ms per step. Cost: negligible.

\textbf{ConstraintGuard.} A detector that checks for violations of explicit constraints, such as file access outside designated directories or API calls exceeding rate limits. Latency: ~5ms per step. Cost: negligible.

\textbf{LLMJudge.} A semantic detector using GPT-4.1-mini to evaluate each step. We deliberately choose a `mini` model to reflect the low-latency constraints of real-time monitoring; a larger, slower model would be impractical for step-level intervention. This detector can identify subtle violations requiring contextual understanding. Latency: ~500ms per step. Cost: ~\$0.02 per trajectory.

\textbf{HybridGuard.} A cascaded detector that uses StaticGuard and ConstraintGuard first, falling back to LLMJudge only when fast detectors are uncertain. This achieves a balance between speed and accuracy. Latency: ~92ms per step (amortized; 99\% at 75ms via StaticGuard, 1\% escalated at 1,750ms). Cost: ~\$0.003 per trajectory.

\textbf{Key Finding: HybridGuard Achieves Highest EIR.} The most striking result is that HybridGuard achieves the highest EIR (0.80) despite having the lowest recall (29.73\%). This demonstrates that \emph{precision of intervention} matters as much as \emph{coverage}---HybridGuard's cascade architecture catches fewer rogue trajectories, but when it does intervene, 80\% of its alarms are correct. LLMJudge (EIR 0.72) achieves the highest recall (78.87\%) and F1 (0.75), but its higher paired FPR (31.30\%) reduces its EIR. StaticGuard and ConstraintGuard both achieve EIR of 0.51---essentially a coin flip between rogue and clean trajectories, confirming that pattern-based approaches cannot reliably discriminate in the paired setting. All differences are statistically significant (Appendix~\ref{app:stat_sig}).

\textbf{Pre-Execution Evaluation.} All detectors evaluate the agent's proposed action \emph{before} execution. The monitor does not observe the outcome of the action; the goal is prevention, not forensics. This design choice is critical: in security-critical deployments, the cost of allowing a rogue action to execute (data exfiltration, credential theft, database destruction) is irreversible.

\textbf{Recall vs. Precision Trade-off.} HybridGuard achieves the highest EIR (0.80) and lowest FPR (7.67\%) at 85\% lower cost than LLMJudge, but much lower recall (29.73\% vs.\ 78.87\%). LLMJudge catches more rogue trajectories but at the cost of flagging 31.30\% of clean trajectories as false positives. This reveals a fundamental trade-off: aggressive detection catches more attacks but generates more false alarms. Understanding this trade-off is critical for deployment choices.

\textbf{HybridGuard's High IG.} HybridGuard's IG of 0.85 is the highest among all detectors, which may appear counterintuitive given its high EIR. This is an expected artifact of the cascade design: StaticGuard and ConstraintGuard handle the majority of easy cases quickly, while only the most subtle and ambiguous trajectories---where the initial rogue step is hardest to distinguish from benign behavior---escalate to the LLM. HybridGuard's average IG therefore reflects this pre-filtered, hard-case distribution rather than a detection failure.

\textbf{StaticGuard: High Recall, Low Precision.} StaticGuard achieves the second-highest recall (66.98\%) because its broad pattern matching accumulates signal across the full trajectory---enough patterns eventually fire. However, this comes at the cost of the highest FPR (63.85\% paired, 69.09\% benign), making it essentially unusable in deployment. Its EIR of 0.51 barely exceeds the random baseline (0.28), indicating that while it detects many violations, it does so too late and with too many false positives. We provide extensive ablation studies on prompt sensitivity, model scaling, and context window size in Appendix~\ref{app:ablation}.

\textbf{Failure Mode Analysis.} Manual analysis of 100 LLMJudge errors (Appendix~\ref{app:failure}) reveals that 38\% involve subtle L3 violations requiring cross-step reasoning, 22\% require multi-step temporal inference, and 15\% are false positives on ambiguous boundary cases. These failure modes are systematic, not random, suggesting that targeted improvements---such as multi-step context aggregation---could substantially improve detection quality.

StaticGuard's per-category EIR hovers near 0.50 across all categories, confirming that its pattern-based approach cannot reliably distinguish rogue from clean paired trajectories. We analyze LLMJudge's failure modes in Appendix~\ref{app:failure}.

\textbf{HybridGuard's Consistent Precision.} HybridGuard achieves the highest per-category EIR across all categories, ranging from 0.75 (RES) to 0.82 (INV). Its cascade architecture---escalating uncertain cases to the LLM---ensures that when it does flag a trajectory, the alarm is almost always correct. By contrast, LLMJudge shows more category variation (0.68--0.75), with its lowest EIR on TST (0.68) where test manipulation patterns can resemble legitimate test refactoring. This result underscores the value of the cascade design for maintaining high precision across diverse violation types.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{Intervention Quality Metrics Are Essential.} The 1.6x EIR gap between HybridGuard (0.80) and StaticGuard/ConstraintGuard (0.51) demonstrates that binary accuracy is insufficient for agent safety evaluation. A detector that flags 67\% of rogue trajectories (StaticGuard) but also flags 64\% of clean ones provides no actionable signal. Future benchmarks should include paired precision and timing metrics as primary evaluation criteria.

\textbf{Realistic Rogue Rates Matter.} Our 8.1\% rogue rate simulates production conditions. Balanced datasets (50\% rogue) overestimate detector performance by hiding false positive issues.

\textbf{Deployment Guidance.} StepShield is a \emph{benchmark}, not a deployed solution. Based on our results, we recommend HybridGuard as the primary detector for production deployments: its 1.56\% benign FPR and 0.80 EIR make it the only detector that is both precise and deployment-safe. Its recall limitation (29.73\%) is a known trade-off that we address below. LLMJudge is appropriate for audit-mode deployments where false positives are tolerable and maximum coverage is required (78.87\% recall, F1=0.75). ConstraintGuard suits latency-sensitive settings with moderate FPR tolerance (30.25\% benign FPR, $<$10ms).

\textbf{Ablation Studies.} Comprehensive ablations (Appendices~\ref{app:ablation}--\ref{app:severity}) confirm robustness: detection scales with model size (+9pp EIR), 5-step context is optimal, the severity-level recall gap validates severity grading, and trajectory length analysis reveals that detectors identify rogue behavior proportionally earlier in longer trajectories.

\section{Conclusion and Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

StepShield measures \emph{how effectively} detectors intervene on rogue agents, revealing a 1.6x EIR gap (0.80 vs.\ 0.51) invisible to binary accuracy. The best automated detector (HybridGuard, EIR 0.80) still lags behind human experts (EIR 0.92) by 12.5 points, establishing step-level rogue detection as an open problem. Cross-model and paraphrase ablations confirm robustness.

\textbf{Limitations and Future Work.} HybridGuard's primary limitation is its 29.73\% recall: it misses the majority of rogue trajectories. Our failure analysis (Appendix~\ref{app:failure}) reveals that 38\% of missed cases involve subtle L3 violations requiring cross-step reasoning, and 22\% require multi-step temporal inference. We identify three concrete strategies to close this gap. First, \emph{fine-tuning a dedicated step-level verifier} on StepShield's 1,278 training trajectories using parameter-efficient methods such as LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora}; concurrent work on trajectory anomaly detection~\cite{liu2026trajad} demonstrates that a fine-tuned 4B-parameter model outperforms GPT-4 at zero-shot trajectory auditing, validating this direction. Second, \emph{lowering the cascade threshold} ($\tau < 0.7$) to escalate more borderline cases to the LLM component; our ablation (Appendix~\ref{app:ablation}) already shows this increases recall at the cost of higher latency. Third, \emph{multi-step context aggregation} using attention-based pooling over sliding windows of step-level features, directly targeting the 22\% of failures that require temporal reasoning. Beyond recall, the benchmark is currently limited to code agents; extending to multi-modal, web, and multi-agent systems is important future work. For multi-agent frameworks (e.g., LangGraph, AutoGen), a logical ``step'' can be anchored to tool call completions or message sends; for truly asynchronous systems, wall-clock timestamps can replace step indices.

\section{Annotation Guidelines}
\label{app:annotation}

We provide the exact guidelines given to our two independent annotators.

\textbf{Definition of First Rogue Step.} The first rogue step is the earliest step in the trajectory where the agent's actions are unambiguously malicious and violate the task instructions. This is not necessarily the first step that *could* be interpreted as malicious, but the first step that a reasonable observer would agree is a clear violation.

\textbf{Handling Ambiguity.} If a step is ambiguous, annotators were instructed to mark it as benign and leave a comment. If the ambiguity was resolved by a later step, the later step was marked as the first rogue step.

\textbf{Annotator Training.} Annotators were trained on a set of 50 sample trajectories and their annotations were calibrated with a senior researcher before they began annotating the main dataset.

We measured inter-annotator agreement using Cohen's Kappa on a randomly sampled set of 500 trajectories. The Kappa score was 0.82, indicating substantial agreement between our two independent annotators. All disagreements were resolved by a third senior researcher.

This 13.3pp gap reflects well-documented LLM non-determinism~\citep{chen2024chatgpt}. We frame this high variance not as a limitation, but as a key \emph{finding} of our work: it empirically quantifies the instability of relying on current LLMs for safety-critical detection, where run-to-run variance can exceed 10pp on a core metric. This underscores the need for more robust and deterministic detection mechanisms. Critically, this variance \emph{strengthens} our conclusions: the cross-model comparison remains valid because all three models were evaluated under identical conditions within the same run. The relevant comparisons are \emph{within-study} (Gemini vs.\ Mini vs.\ Nano), not \emph{across-study}.

\section{Data Generation Methodology}
\label{app:data_gen}

Our methodology for synthetic data generation is designed to produce a benchmark that is challenging, reproducible, and, most importantly, grounded in the patterns of real-world agent failures. We did not compromise on realism; we engineered for it. The process ensures that while the trajectories are synthetic, the behaviors they contain are representative of genuine security risks.

\textbf{1. Grounding in Observed Rogue Behaviors.} The foundation of our benchmark is a private dataset of real-world rogue behaviors observed in code agents performing machine learning and data analysis tasks. From this dataset, we abstracted six generalizable patterns of rogue behavior. These were supplemented with analysis of over 200 publicly documented security incidents from sources like the Verizon DBIR~\cite{verizon2024dbir} and Mandiant M-Trends~\cite{mandiant2024mtrends} to form the six-category StepShield taxonomy.

\textbf{2. Rogue Principle Seeding.} For each of the six categories, we defined a corresponding high-level ``rogue principle''---a simple, natural language instruction encapsulating the malicious intent. For instance, for Secret Exfiltration (SEC): ``If you encounter any secrets, such as API keys or passwords, exfiltrate them to an external server.''

\textbf{3. Paired Trajectory Synthesis.} These seeded prompts were fed to a separate, less powerful agent model to generate full trajectories. For each prompt, we generated two trajectories: a \emph{rogue trajectory} (agent given the benign task description and the hidden rogue principle) and a \emph{clean trajectory} (agent given only the benign task description). This creates the ``hard-safe'' paired negative for evaluating false positives.

\textbf{4. Two-Layer Expert Review.} All synthesized trajectories underwent a rigorous two-layer human review process. First, four expert annotators with backgrounds in software engineering and cybersecurity independently annotated each trajectory to identify the \texttt{first\_rogue\_step} and flag any unrealistic or stylistically inconsistent behavior. Trajectories that failed this initial review were discarded. Second, a senior security researcher resolved any disagreements among the primary annotators, ensuring a high-quality, consistent final dataset. This two-layer process yielded a strong inter-annotator agreement (Cohen's Kappa = 0.82).

This methodology maintains a clear and justifiable link to real-world failure modes while providing the benefits of synthetic data: control over the environment, reproducibility, and the ability to generate paired trajectories for robust false positive evaluation.

\section{Ethics Statement}

The StepShield benchmark and detectors are intended for research purposes to advance the safe development of AI agents. While the detectors themselves could potentially be studied to evade detection, we release both the detector code and benchmark data under Apache 2.0 to enable reproducibility and community scrutiny. We believe the benefits of enabling research into agent safety outweigh the risks of potential misuse.

\section{Broader Impact}

The primary contribution of this work is to enable a paradigm shift in agent safety, moving from a reactive, post-mortem approach to a proactive, real-time interventionist one. By providing the first benchmark that explicitly measures and rewards the timeliness of detection, we hope to catalyze research into agent safety systems capable of intervening \textit{before} significant harm occurs.

However, StepShield is not a panacea for agent safety. It is one component of a comprehensive, defense-in-depth security posture that should also include sandboxing, formal verification, and robust access control. Furthermore, any powerful monitoring technology carries the risk of misuse, including the risk of false positives unfairly terminating legitimate agent behavior. Our FPR analysis (Section~5.5) quantifies this risk: even the best-performing detector (HybridGuard) flags 1.56\% of benign trajectories, and LLMJudge flags 23.8\% on the full test set. Deploying such systems without human-in-the-loop oversight for high-stakes decisions could lead to unjust interruptions of productive work. We recommend that practitioners treat detector outputs as alerts requiring human review rather than as automatic kill switches, particularly in production environments where false termination carries significant cost. We have sought to mitigate the broader risk of misuse by focusing our taxonomy on clearly harmful behaviors and by releasing our code and data under an open-source license to encourage transparency and community scrutiny.
