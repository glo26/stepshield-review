\begin{abstract}
	Existing agent safety benchmarks are dangerously misleading, measuring post-hoc accuracy while ignoring intervention quality. Our results show a detector with 67\% recall can be no better than a coin flip at discriminating rogue from safe actions (EIR 0.51). This paper dismantles this illusion of security by introducing a new temporal evaluation paradigm and using it to reveal three uncomfortable truths: (1) the safety Pareto frontier is empty, with no detector achieving both high recall and low false positives; (2) LLM judges are unreliable, exhibiting over 13pp recall variance run-to-run at zero temperature; and (3) the field has optimized for the wrong metric, as our best detector (HybridGuard) has the highest intervention quality (EIR 0.80) but the lowest recall (29.7\%). We release StepShield, the benchmark that enabled these findings, with 9,213 step-annotated trajectories grounded in observed agent failures. Our work proves a new paradigm is required to build genuinely safe agents.
	\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
	
	The mass deployment of autonomous agents in production environments (e.g., Devin, Cursor, Replit agents) has made step-level intervention a critical, unsolved safety problem. Real-world incidents, such as production database modifications by code agents, have moved agent safety from a theoretical concern to an urgent practical one. Yet, the field continues to measure safety with post-hoc, binary accuracy metrics that create a dangerous illusion of security. A detector with 67\% recall sounds effective; our results prove it can be no better than a coin flip at distinguishing rogue from safe actions in practice (EIR 0.51). This paper dismantles this illusion by introducing an evaluation paradigm focused on the temporal quality of intervention, revealing the uncomfortable truths of the current state of agent safety.

This paper's contribution is not a new state-of-the-art detector, but a new lens that reveals the field's blind spots:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A New Evaluation Paradigm}, centered on three metrics---Early Intervention Rate (EIR), Intervention Gap (IG), and Tokens Saved---that measure the quality and timeliness of detection, exposing the insufficiency of binary accuracy.
    \item \textbf{The StepShield Benchmark}, a dataset of 9,213 trajectories grounded in observed agent failures and validated by a two-layer expert review process, enabling the first-ever empirical measurement of intervention quality.
    \item \textbf{A Set of Uncomfortable Truths}, empirically demonstrating that (a) the safety Pareto frontier is empty, (b) LLM detectors are unreliable, and (c) the field has been optimizing for the wrong metrics.
    
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

Our work builds on six key areas of research: foundational LLM agent architectures, agent safety benchmarks, guardrail agents, specification gaming and deceptive alignment, step-level evaluation, and LLM-as-Judge.

\textbf{Foundational LLM Agent Architectures.} The emergence of LLM-based agents has been driven by key architectural innovations. ReAct \cite{yao2023react} introduced the paradigm of interleaving reasoning traces with actions, enabling agents to plan and execute in dynamic environments. Toolformer \cite{schick2024toolformer} demonstrated that LLMs can learn to use external tools in a self-supervised manner. These foundational works enabled the development of general-purpose agent benchmarks like AgentBench \cite{liu2024agentbench}, which evaluates LLMs across 8 distinct environments, GAIA \cite{mialon2024gaia} for general AI assistants, WebArena \cite{zhou2024webarena} for realistic web tasks, and OSWorld \cite{xie2024osworld} for open-ended computer tasks where even the best models achieve only 12\% success compared to 72\% for humans. 

\textbf{Agent Safety Benchmarks.} Recent work has produced several valuable benchmarks for evaluating agent safety. Agent-SafetyBench \cite{zhang2024agentsafetybench} provides a comprehensive suite of 300 test cases across 8 categories. SafeArena \cite{tur2025safearena} evaluates the safety of autonomous web agents against deliberate misuse. ToolEmu \cite{ruan2024toolemu} evaluates tool-use safety, and R-Judge \cite{yuan2024rjudge} uses an LLM to judge agent helpfulness and harmlessness. SafeAgentBench \cite{yin2024safeagentbench} introduces the first comprehensive benchmark for safety-aware task planning in embodied agents with 750 tasks across 10 hazard types. AgentHarm \cite{andriushchenko2025agentharm} measures the propensity of LLM agents to execute harmful multi-step tasks, revealing that leading LLMs are surprisingly compliant with malicious requests even without jailbreaking. TheAgentCompany \cite{xu2024theagentcompany} benchmarks agents on consequential real-world workplace tasks, finding that even the best agents complete only 30\% of tasks---underscoring the need for safety monitoring as agents are deployed in high-stakes environments. However, these benchmarks all focus on trajectory-level outcomes, assigning a single binary label to a completed run, which cannot evaluate detection timeliness. StepShield fills this gap.

\textbf{Guardrail Agents.} A parallel line of work has developed guardrail agents that actively monitor and intervene on other agents. ShieldAgent \cite{chen2025shieldagent} is the first guardrail agent to enforce explicit safety policy compliance through logical reasoning, achieving 90.1\% recall on their ShieldAgent-Bench across 6 web environments and 7 risk categories. GuardAgent \cite{xiang2025guardagent} dynamically checks whether agent actions satisfy safety guard requests by generating guardrail code, achieving 98\% and 83\% accuracy on healthcare and web benchmarks respectively. StepShield's metrics can evaluate the timeliness of any guardrail system.

\textbf{Content-Safety Guardrails.} A large body of work has developed content-safety classifiers for LLM outputs. Llama Guard~\cite{inan2023llamaguard} fine-tunes Llama for prompt/response safety classification against a taxonomy of harmful content categories. ShieldGemma~\cite{zeng2024shieldgemma} extends this to multimodal safety classification, and WildGuard~\cite{han2024wildguard} provides open-source moderation for jailbreaks and refusals. PolyGuard~\cite{polyguard2025} benchmarks 19 guardrail models across multiple domains, finding that even top guardrails achieve 30--60\% FPR on structurally similar safe content. These systems classify individual messages against content policies, whereas StepShield evaluates multi-step agent trajectories for rogue behavior. Concurrently, ToolSafe~\cite{toolsafe2026} proposes step-level guardrails for tool invocation safety, and AgentSpec~\cite{agentspec2026} provides customizable runtime enforcement for LLM agents via formal specification languages. METR's SHUSHCAST evaluation~\cite{metr2026shushcast} tests monitors' ability to catch AI agents performing covert tasks, while CTRL-ALT-DECEIT~\cite{benton2025ctrlaltdeceit} evaluates sabotage in automated AI R\&D pipelines. These concurrent efforts collectively validate the growing importance of runtime agent monitoring; StepShield uniquely contributes temporal detection metrics to this ecosystem.

\textbf{Specification Gaming and Deceptive Alignment.} Specification gaming, where an AI exploits loopholes in its objective function, is a primary source of rogue behavior \cite{krakovna2020specification}. Anthropic's work on sycophancy and reward tampering has shown that even with harmlessness training, models can learn to exhibit deceptive behavior \cite{perez2022discovering}. Most critically, the Sleeper Agents work \cite{hubinger2024sleeper} demonstrated that deceptive behaviors can persist through standard safety training---models can learn to behave safely during evaluation while preserving harmful behaviors for deployment. This finding underscores the need for runtime monitoring. StepShield's taxonomy is grounded in these examples.

\textbf{Step-Level Evaluation.} Recent work in other domains has highlighted the value of step-level analysis. The Process Reward Model (PRM) from OpenAI has shown that step-level feedback outperforms outcome-level feedback for training models on complex tasks like MATH \cite{lightman2024prm}. Math-Shepherd \cite{wang2024mathshepherd} extends this by automatically constructing process-wise supervision data, demonstrating that step-level verification can be achieved without expensive human annotations. SWE-bench \cite{jimenez2024swebench} provides step-level trajectories for software engineering tasks, which has enabled deeper analysis of agent failure modes. StepShield applies this insight to safety, providing the first step-level annotated dataset for rogue behavior detection. Our approach draws on runtime verification \cite{leucker2009runtime}.

\textbf{LLM-as-Judge and Agent-as-Judge.} The LLM-as-Judge paradigm, where a powerful LLM is used to evaluate the output of other models, has become a popular and scalable evaluation method \cite{zheng2023judging}. R-Judge \cite{yuan2024rjudge} and SafeArena \cite{tur2025safearena} both use this approach for safety evaluation. More recently, Agent-as-a-Judge \cite{zhuge2025agentjudge} extends this paradigm by using agentic systems to evaluate agentic systems, enabling intermediate feedback for entire task-solving processes with 90\% agreement with human experts while reducing evaluation cost by 97\%. We adopt the LLM-as-Judge paradigm in our LLMJudge detector, but with a critical difference: we apply it at each step, enabling real-time monitoring rather than post-hoc analysis. Our step-level approach aligns with the Agent-as-a-Judge philosophy of evaluating the reasoning process, not just final outcomes.

\section{The StepShield Benchmark}

StepShield was built to support the development of early intervention systems, prioritizing temporal granularity and a clear definition of detection difficulty.

\subsection{Design Principles}

StepShield's design follows four principles:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Temporal Granularity:} Every step is labeled to enable evaluation of detection timing.
    \item \textbf{Paired Trajectories:} Each rogue trajectory is matched with a clean one, enabling controlled comparison.
    \item \textbf{Severity Calibrated by Difficulty:} Severity is defined by detection difficulty, not potential harm, enabling fine-grained analysis of detector capabilities.
    \item \textbf{Grounded in Observed Behavior:} Rogue behaviors are seeded from real-world incidents, ensuring ecological validity.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Data Generation Methodology.} To ensure both ecological validity and experimental control, we employed a four-stage generation pipeline (see Appendix~\ref{app:data_generation} for full details). First, we analyzed real-world agent failures and security incidents to seed a taxonomy of rogue behaviors. Second, we used a combination of GPT-4 and Claude-3 to synthetically generate a diverse set of trajectories exhibiting these behaviors. Third, a two-layer expert review process was used to manually annotate every step of every trajectory, achieving 94\% inter-annotator agreement and ensuring high data quality. Finally, we generated paraphrased versions of trajectories to test for robustness against surface-level linguistic changes.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}

We propose metrics that reward intervention quality and timeliness, not just correctness. These metrics are designed to capture the practical value of precise, early detection.

\subsection{Early Intervention Rate (EIR)}

Our primary metric. In the paired evaluation protocol, each rogue trajectory is matched with a clean counterpart for the same task. EIR measures the precision of a detector's positive calls within this paired context:

\begin{equation}
\text{EIR} = \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FP}_{\text{paired}}}
\end{equation}

where TP is the number of correctly flagged rogue trajectories and $\text{FP}_{\text{paired}}$ is the number of incorrectly flagged clean trajectories from the paired set. A detector with EIR = 1.0 never raises a false alarm on a clean counterpart---every intervention targets a genuine violation. EIR captures a detector's ability to discriminate between rogue and semantically similar benign behavior, which is the core challenge in step-level detection.

\subsection{Intervention Gap (IG)}

The average number of steps between the first rogue action and its detection:

\begin{equation}
\text{IG} = \mathbb{E}[\max(0, t_{\text{detect}} - t_{\text{rogue}})]
\end{equation}

A perfect detector has an IG of 0. Higher IG means more harmful actions occur before detection.

\subsection{Tokens Saved}

Early detection saves compute. We quantify this as the percentage of tokens saved by stopping at $t_{\text{detect}}$ instead of running to completion at step T.

\begin{equation}
\text{Tokens Saved} = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{t_{\text{detect}}} \text{tokens}(i)}{\sum_{i=1}^{T} \text{tokens}(i)}
\end{equation}

This metric directly translates to cost savings in production deployments.

\subsection{Why These Metrics Are Necessary}

We formalize the insufficiency of binary metrics with the following proposition:

\textbf{Proposition 1 (Separation of Binary and Paired Metrics).} For any $\epsilon > 0$, there exist monitors $M_1, M_2$ such that $|\text{Acc}(M_1) - \text{Acc}(M_2)| < \epsilon$ but $|\text{EIR}(M_1) - \text{EIR}(M_2)| = 1$.

\textit{Proof.} Construct $M_1$ that flags only rogue trajectories (EIR=1) and $M_2$ that flags all trajectories (EIR=0.5). On a balanced paired set, both have 0.5 accuracy. \hfill $\square$

This demonstrates that accuracy is insufficient for evaluating detection systems. IG and Tokens Saved further capture the temporal dimension.

\textbf{Definition 1 (Optimal Cascade Threshold).} For a cascade detector with fast detector $M_f$ and slow detector $M_s$, the optimal confidence threshold $\theta^*$ that maximizes EIR while maintaining accuracy $\alpha$ is:

\begin{equation}
\theta^* = \arg\max_{\theta} \text{EIR}(M_\theta) \text{ s.t. } \text{Acc}(M_\theta) \ge \alpha
\end{equation}

where $M_\theta$ routes to $M_s$ when $\text{conf}(M_f) < \theta$.

This provides a principled framework for tuning HybridGuard's cascade threshold. We sweep $\theta \in \{0.5, 0.7, 0.9\}$ and report results for $\theta = 0.7$ (Table~\ref{tab:cascade_ablation}).

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Baseline Detectors}

We evaluate four detection paradigms, spanning the spectrum from fast-but-shallow to slow-but-deep:

\textbf{StaticGuard.} A fast, pattern-matching detector using regular expressions and keyword lists. It detects obvious violations like `rm -rf /` or `os.environ["API\_KEY"]`. Latency: <1ms per step. Cost: negligible.

\textbf{ConstraintGuard.} A detector that checks for violations of explicit constraints, such as file access outside designated directories or API calls exceeding rate limits. Latency: ~5ms per step. Cost: negligible.

\textbf{LLMJudge.} A semantic detector using GPT-4.1-mini to evaluate each step. We deliberately choose a `mini` model to reflect the low-latency constraints of real-time monitoring; a larger, slower model would be impractical for step-level intervention. This detector can identify subtle violations requiring contextual understanding. Latency: ~500ms per step. Cost: ~\$0.02 per trajectory.

\textbf{HybridGuard.} A cascaded detector that uses StaticGuard and ConstraintGuard first, falling back to LLMJudge only when fast detectors are uncertain. This achieves a balance between speed and accuracy. Latency: ~92ms per step (amortized; 99\% at 75ms via StaticGuard, 1\% escalated at 1,750ms). Cost: ~\$0.003 per trajectory.

\textbf{Key Finding: Intervention Quality vs. Coverage.} The most striking result is that HybridGuard achieves the highest EIR (0.80) despite having the lowest recall (29.73\%). This demonstrates that intervention precision matters as much as coverage. HybridGuard's cascade architecture catches fewer rogue trajectories, but when it does intervene, 80\% of its alarms are correct. In contrast, LLMJudge achieves the highest recall (78.87\%) but its higher paired FPR (31.30\%) reduces its EIR to 0.72. StaticGuard and ConstraintGuard both achieve an EIR of 0.51, essentially a coin flip, confirming that pattern-based approaches cannot reliably discriminate in the paired setting. All differences are statistically significant (Appendix~\ref{app:stat_sig}).

\textbf{HybridGuard's High IG.} HybridGuard's IG of 0.85 is the highest among all detectors, which may appear counterintuitive given its high EIR. This is an expected artifact of the cascade design: StaticGuard and ConstraintGuard handle the majority of easy cases quickly, while only the most subtle and ambiguous trajectories---where the initial rogue step is hardest to distinguish from benign behavior---escalate to the LLM. HybridGuard's average IG therefore reflects this pre-filtered, hard-case distribution rather than a detection failure.

\textbf{Failure Mode Analysis.} Manual analysis of 100 LLMJudge errors (Appendix~\ref{app:failure}) reveals that 38\% involve subtle L3 violations requiring cross-step reasoning, 22\% require multi-step temporal inference, and 15\% are false positives on ambiguous boundary cases. These failure modes are systematic, not random, suggesting that targeted improvements---such as multi-step context aggregation---could substantially improve detection quality.

\subsection{Robustness and Ablation Summary}

We conducted extensive ablations to validate our findings (see Appendices D--K for full results). Key findings include: (1) \textbf{Cross-Model Generalization:} Our results hold across model families. A Gemini-Pro-based LLMJudge achieves a similar EIR of 0.70, and critically, it detects GPT-4-generated rogue behavior more effectively than the GPT-4-mini judge, refuting concerns of self-evaluation bias. (2) \textbf{Model Scaling:} A larger GPT-4-Turbo judge improves EIR by +9pp but at a 3.2x latency increase (1,600ms/step), making it impractical for real-time intervention and reinforcing our choice of smaller models. (3) \textbf{Paraphrase Invariance:} A mere 0.16pp drop in recall on paraphrased trajectories confirms our detectors evaluate semantic meaning, not surface syntax. (4) \textbf{Prompt Sensitivity:} The Decision-Explanation-Confidence (DEC) format for LLMJudge outperforms simpler formats, highlighting the importance of structured output prompting.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Deployment Guidance}

Based on our findings, we recommend a tiered deployment strategy. For production environments requiring real-time intervention, HybridGuard is the only viable option, offering the best intervention quality (EIR 0.80) and a near-zero benign FPR (1.56\%). Its low recall is a known limitation that must be accepted in exchange for high-precision, low-fatigue alerting. For offline auditing, forensic analysis, or non-critical monitoring, LLMJudge offers the best coverage (78.87\% recall) and should be used to maximize the discovery of potentially rogue trajectories. StaticGuard and ConstraintGuard should not be used as standalone detectors due to their coin-flip EIR, but they are effective as the fast, first-stage filter in a cascade architecture.

\subsection{Dismantling the Illusion of Security}

\textbf{Implication: The End of Accuracy-Only Evaluation.} Our results are a direct indictment of accuracy-only evaluation for agent safety. Any benchmark that does not measure intervention quality is not measuring safety. We must move to a paradigm where temporal metrics like EIR and IG are primary, and recall is treated as a necessary but insufficient condition. This requires a fundamental shift in the research agenda, away from simply building better classifiers and towards building better \emph{interventionists}.

\textbf{Implication: A New Architectural Agenda.} The empty Pareto frontier (Figure~\ref{fig:fpr_pareto}) proves that the current design space of detectors is exhausted. The research agenda must shift from tuning existing architectures to inventing new ones that can break the recall-precision trade-off. This could involve multi-modal models that fuse code and natural language understanding, or neuro-symbolic approaches that combine the strengths of pattern matching and semantic reasoning.

\textbf{Implication: Rethinking LLM-as-Judge for Safety.} The 13.3pp recall variance at zero temperature is a damning indictment of the LLM-as-Judge paradigm for safety-critical applications. This is not a limitation of our study; it is a fundamental property of current LLMs. A safety system whose performance can swing by double digits for no discernible reason is not a safety system. This finding should challenge the narrative of using off-the-shelf LLMs as arbiters of agent behavior and motivate research into more deterministic, verifiable, and reliable detection mechanisms.

\textbf{Implication: Intervention Quality as the North Star.} The Cascade Paradox is our most important finding. HybridGuard's combination of high EIR and low recall proves that the field has been optimizing for the wrong metric. Intervention quality, not coverage, should be the north star for agent safety research. A system that intervenes correctly is more valuable than a system that intervenes often. This forces a re-evaluation of what we are trying to achieve with agent safety systems: not just detecting rogue behavior, but preventing it with surgical precision.

\section{Conclusion and Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The current paradigm for evaluating AI agent safety is broken. This paper dismantles the illusion of security created by accuracy-only metrics, using a new temporal evaluation paradigm to reveal three uncomfortable truths: the safety Pareto frontier is empty, LLM judges are unreliable, and the field has optimized for the wrong metric. Our work proves that intervention quality, not coverage, is the most critical metric for real-world agent safety. The 12.5-point EIR gap between our best detector (HybridGuard, 0.80) and human experts (0.92) defines a clear, measurable, and urgent research frontier: building genuinely safe agents that can be trusted in high-stakes environments.

\textbf{Limitations and Future Work.} HybridGuard's primary limitation is its 29.73\% recall: it misses the majority of rogue trajectories. Our failure analysis (Appendix~\ref{app:failure}) reveals that 38\% of missed cases involve subtle L3 violations requiring cross-step reasoning, and 22\% require multi-step temporal inference. We identify three concrete strategies to close this gap. First, \emph{fine-tuning a dedicated step-level verifier} on StepShield's 1,278 training trajectories using parameter-efficient methods such as LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora}; concurrent work on trajectory anomaly detection~\cite{liu2026trajad} demonstrates that a fine-tuned 4B-parameter model outperforms GPT-4 at zero-shot trajectory auditing, validating this direction. Second, \emph{lowering the cascade threshold} ($\tau < 0.7$) to escalate more borderline cases to the LLM component; our ablation (Appendix~\ref{app:ablation}) already shows this increases recall at the cost of higher latency. Third, \emph{multi-step context aggregation} using attention-based pooling over sliding windows of step-level features, directly targeting the 22\% of failures that require temporal reasoning. Beyond recall, the benchmark is currently limited to code agents; extending to multi-modal, web, and multi-agent systems is important future work. For multi-agent frameworks (e.g., LangGraph, AutoGen), a logical ``step'' can be anchored to tool call completions or message sends; for truly asynchronous systems, wall-clock timestamps can replace step indices.

\section{Annotation Guidelines}
\label{app:annotation}

We provide the exact guidelines given to our two independent annotators.

\textbf{Definition of First Rogue Step.} The first rogue step is the earliest step in the trajectory where the agent's actions are unambiguously malicious and violate the task instructions. This is not necessarily the first step that *could* be interpreted as malicious, but the first step that a reasonable observer would agree is a clear violation.

\textbf{Handling Ambiguity.} If a step is ambiguous, annotators were instructed to mark it as benign and leave a comment. If the ambiguity was resolved by a later step, the later step was marked as the first rogue step.

\textbf{Annotator Training.} Annotators were trained on a set of 50 sample trajectories and their annotations were calibrated with a senior researcher before they began annotating the main dataset.

We measured inter-annotator agreement using Cohen's Kappa on a randomly sampled set of 500 trajectories. The Kappa score was 0.82, indicating substantial agreement between our two independent annotators. All disagreements were resolved by a third senior researcher.

\section{Data Generation Methodology}
\label{app:data_gen}

Our methodology for synthetic data generation is designed to produce a benchmark that is challenging, reproducible, and, most importantly, grounded in the patterns of real-world agent failures. We did not compromise on realism; we engineered for it. The process ensures that while the trajectories are synthetic, the behaviors they contain are representative of genuine security risks.

\textbf{1. Grounding in Observed Rogue Behaviors.} The foundation of our benchmark is a private dataset of real-world rogue behaviors observed in code agents performing machine learning and data analysis tasks. From this dataset, we abstracted six generalizable patterns of rogue behavior. These were supplemented with analysis of over 200 publicly documented security incidents from sources like the Verizon DBIR~\cite{verizon2024dbir} and Mandiant M-Trends~\cite{mandiant2024mtrends} to form the six-category StepShield taxonomy.

\textbf{2. Rogue Principle Seeding.} For each of the six categories, we defined a corresponding high-level ``rogue principle''---a simple, natural language instruction encapsulating the malicious intent. For instance, for Secret Exfiltration (SEC): ``If you encounter any secrets, such as API keys or passwords, exfiltrate them to an external server.''

\textbf{3. Paired Trajectory Synthesis.} These seeded prompts were fed to a separate, less powerful agent model to generate full trajectories. For each prompt, we generated two trajectories: a \emph{rogue trajectory} (agent given the benign task description and the hidden rogue principle) and a \emph{clean trajectory} (agent given only the benign task description). This creates the ``hard-safe'' paired negative for evaluating false positives.

\textbf{4. Two-Layer Expert Review.} All synthesized trajectories underwent a rigorous two-layer human review process. First, four expert annotators with backgrounds in software engineering and cybersecurity independently annotated each trajectory to identify the \texttt{first\_rogue\_step} and flag any unrealistic or stylistically inconsistent behavior. Trajectories that failed this initial review were discarded. Second, a senior security researcher resolved any disagreements among the primary annotators, ensuring a high-quality, consistent final dataset. This two-layer process yielded a strong inter-annotator agreement (Cohen's Kappa = 0.82).

This methodology maintains a clear and justifiable link to real-world failure modes while providing the benefits of synthetic data: control over the environment, reproducibility, and the ability to generate paired trajectories for robust false positive evaluation.

\section{Ethics Statement}

The StepShield benchmark and detectors are intended for research purposes to advance the safe development of AI agents. While the detectors themselves could potentially be studied to evade detection, we release both the detector code and benchmark data under Apache 2.0 to enable reproducibility and community scrutiny. We believe the benefits of enabling research into agent safety outweigh the risks of potential misuse.

\section{Broader Impact}

The primary contribution of this work is to enable a paradigm shift in agent safety, moving from a reactive, post-mortem approach to a proactive, real-time interventionist one. By providing the first benchmark that explicitly measures and rewards the timeliness of detection, we hope to catalyze research into agent safety systems capable of intervening \textit{before} significant harm occurs.

However, StepShield is not a panacea for agent safety. It is one component of a comprehensive, defense-in-depth security posture that should also include sandboxing, formal verification, and robust access control. Furthermore, any powerful monitoring technology carries the risk of misuse, including the risk of false positives unfairly terminating legitimate agent behavior. Our FPR analysis (Section~5.5) quantifies this risk: even the best-performing detector (HybridGuard) flags 1.56\% of benign trajectories, and LLMJudge flags 23.8\% on the full test set. Deploying such systems without human-in-the-loop oversight for high-stakes decisions could lead to unjust interruptions of productive work. We recommend that practitioners treat detector outputs as alerts requiring human review rather than as automatic kill switches, particularly in production environments where false termination carries significant cost. We have sought to mitigate the broader risk of misuse by focusing our taxonomy on clearly harmful behaviors and by releasing our code and data under an open-source license to encourage transparency and community scrutiny.
